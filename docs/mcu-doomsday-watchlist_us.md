## Three Red Threads

These are the storylines that run through the entire list and converge in Doomsday.

**The Face** — Robert Downey Jr. returns, not as Tony Stark but as Victor von Doom. For anyone who has grown up alongside Stark over the years, that is an emotional gut punch. Homecoming, Far From Home and Endgame lay that foundation.

**The Mechanics** — How do timelines collide? How does a universe die? Loki explains the TVA's rules, MoM introduces Incursions, No Way Home shows the first real fracture, and Deadpool & Wolverine fleshes out the concept of Anchor Beings. Without these four, you won't understand the mechanics of Doomsday.

**The Teams** — Doomsday is not a film about one team. The Avengers, the Thunderbolts, the Fantastic Four and the Young Avengers are all built up separately before ultimately coming together. Every title in this list contributes a piece to that larger front.

## Block 1 — The Legacy: The Foundation

*8 titles · ~19 hours*

The foundation rests on one question: what are we about to lose when Tony Stark is gone? This block builds the world as it was — magical, technological, political — so that the blow of Endgame truly lands. You learn the rules of the MCU at the moment they are still stable. Magic exists, Vibranium shapes global power, the Quantum Realm is accessible. All of these elements return. Take your time with this block. It is the base on which everything rests.

---

### 1. Doctor Strange
*Film · 2h 15m*

- **What** — Neurosurgeon Stephen Strange loses the use of his hands in a car accident and finds his way to the mystic arts.
- **Why** — The introduction of magic as a serious force in the MCU. Other dimensions, the Sanctum Sanctorum, the Eye of Agamotto — without this film, MoM is incomprehensible. Strange is one of the central figures in Doomsday.
- **When** — Before Infinity War. The magic logic needs to be in place.
- **Who** — Benedict Cumberbatch as Stephen Strange, Tilda Swinton as the Ancient One.

---

### 2. Spider-Man: Homecoming
*Film · 2h 13m*

- **What** — Peter Parker tries to earn his place as a superhero in the shadow of Tony Stark, while keeping his high school life together.
- **Why** — The Stark/Parker dynamic is emotionally the core of Doomsday's "face" storyline. When Doctor Doom appears wearing Stark's face, it's Peter's reaction that matters. Homecoming provides that context.
- **When** — Before Infinity War and Far From Home.
- **Who** — Tom Holland as Peter Parker, Robert Downey Jr. as Tony Stark.

---

### 3. Black Panther
*Film · 2h 14m*

- **What** — T'Challa returns to Wakanda after his father's death and must defend his throne while a dangerous rival wants to open the nation up to war.
- **Why** — Wakanda is a global player in Doomsday. Vibranium, the technology, the political autonomy. Shuri and Namor — both confirmed for Doomsday — become easier to understand in later films with this one as their foundation.
- **When** — Before Infinity War.
- **Who** — Chadwick Boseman as T'Challa, Letitia Wright as Shuri, Michael B. Jordan as Killmonger.

---

### 4. Black Widow
*Film · 2h 14m*

- **What** — Natasha Romanoff confronts her past as she dismantles a dangerous programme that controls dozens of trained widows.
- **Why** — The introduction of Yelena Belova. She is one of the central figures in Thunderbolts and returns in Doomsday. Red Guardian also appears here for the first time. The family dynamic between Natasha and Yelena gives the Thunderbolts storyline its emotional depth.
- **When** — Set before Infinity War, but watch it after Endgame in this order.
- **Who** — Scarlett Johansson as Natasha, Florence Pugh as Yelena, David Harbour as Red Guardian.

---

### 5. Ant-Man and the Wasp ★
*Film · 1h 58m*

- **What** — Scott Lang and Hope van Dyne enter the Quantum Realm to rescue Hope's mother, while an unstable woman tries to stop them.
- **Why** — The Quantum Realm is the key to Endgame's time travel solution and the foundation of the multiverse's mechanics. Ghost plays a role later in Thunderbolts. The post-credits scene connects directly to Infinity War.
- **When** — Before Ragnarok and Infinity War.
- **Who** — Paul Rudd as Scott Lang, Evangeline Lilly as Hope van Dyne, Hannah John-Kamen as Ghost.

---

### 6. Thor: Ragnarok ★
*Film · 2h 10m*

- **What** — Thor discovers Odin has vanished, loses Mjolnir to Hela and must fight his way back through Sakaar to save Asgard.
- **Why** — The direct bridge to Infinity War. Ragnarok explains why Thor begins his fight against Thanos without his hammer, with one eye and with the survivors of Asgard aboard a refugee ship. Loki's choice involving the Tesseract makes the opening scene of Infinity War logical and emotionally heavier. It also shows that even the realms of gods can fall — exactly the kind of power vacuum that Doom will later exploit.
- **When** — Directly before Infinity War. The final scene runs straight into the opening scene of Infinity War.
- **Who** — Chris Hemsworth as Thor, Tom Hiddleston as Loki, Cate Blanchett as Hela, Tessa Thompson as Valkyrie, Mark Ruffalo as Hulk.

---

### 7. Avengers: Infinity War ★
*Film · 2h 29m*

- **What** — Thanos collects the Infinity Stones to destroy half of all life in the universe. The Avengers fail.
- **Why** — The first true multiverse-scale threat. The destruction Thanos unleashes is the benchmark against which all later threats — including Doom — are measured. You need to feel what it is like to lose.
- **When** — After all of the above films.
- **Who** — The full Avengers ensemble, Josh Brolin as Thanos.

---

### 8. Avengers: Endgame ★
*Film · 3h 1m*

- **What** — Five years after the Snap, the surviving Avengers attempt to retrieve the Infinity Stones through time travel. Tony Stark sacrifices himself.
- **Why** — Everything. Stark's sacrifice is the emotional anchor of the entire story through to Doomsday. The time travel creates the multiverse fractures that drive the entire Multiverse Saga. Sam Wilson becomes Captain America here. Endgame is the end of one era and the beginning of everything that follows.
- **When** — Closing out Block 1.
- **Who** — The full ensemble. Robert Downey Jr. as Tony Stark, Chris Evans as Steve Rogers, Anthony Mackie as Sam Wilson.

## Block 2 — The New Guard

*6 titles · ~22 hours*

Stark is dead. Rogers is old. Thor is somewhere in space. The world needs new heroes, but those heroes aren't ready yet. This block is about the transition — about people picking up a mantle that weighs more than expected. Sam Wilson wrestles with Steve Rogers' shield. Wanda Maximoff loses control of her grief and discovers a power she barely understands. Hawkeye trains his successor. The Avengers as an institution no longer exists, but the threat does. This block is slower than Block 1, but the emotional groundwork laid here is essential for the finale.

---

### 9. Spider-Man: Far From Home ★
*Film · 2h 9m*

- **What** — Peter Parker tries to go on holiday after Endgame, but is pulled back into action. A new threat appears — or seems to.
- **Why** — The direct emotional aftermath of Endgame. Peter is grieving Stark. The world is grieving Stark. The film poses the question that Doomsday will answer: who takes on that role now? The post-credits scene has direct consequences for No Way Home.
- **When** — Directly after Endgame, before WandaVision.
- **Who** — Tom Holland as Peter Parker, Jake Gyllenhaal as Mysterio.

---

### 10. WandaVision ★
*Series · ~5 hours*

- **What** — Wanda Maximoff, consumed by grief, creates a fictional reality in the town of Westview, modelled on old sitcoms. The outside world tries to break through.
- **Why** — The birth of the Scarlet Witch. Chaos Magic is fully displayed here for the first time. Without WandaVision, Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness makes no sense — and without MoM, you won't understand Incursions. This is the emotional and technical foundation of the entire magic storyline.
- **When** — After Far From Home, before Falcon & The Winter Soldier.
- **Who** — Elizabeth Olsen as Wanda/Scarlet Witch, Paul Bettany as Vision.

## Block 3 — The Multiverse Fracture: The Road to Doom

*8 titles · ~26 hours*

This is the technical heart of the watch guide. The Multiverse Saga is not just about more heroes — it is about the fundamental instability of reality itself. Timelines branch, universes collide, and those who try to maintain order lose control. This block explains the mechanics that make Doomsday possible: the TVA, Incursions, the concept of Anchor Beings, and the idea that entire timelines can be erased. Watch this block carefully. The rules explained here are the rules of the endgame.

---

### 15. Loki — Season 1 & 2 ★
*Series · ~10 hours*

- **What** — A variant version of Loki is captured by the Time Variance Authority. He discovers the TVA is hiding far more than it lets on.
- **Why** — The single most essential series for Doomsday. The TVA, the concept of variants, the Sacred Timeline, and what happens when timelines fall out of balance — all of it is explained here. Season 2 brings Loki to a sacrifice that fundamentally changes the structure of the multiverse. Loki is confirmed for Doomsday.
- **When** — Start of Block 3, before everything that follows.
- **Who** — Tom Hiddleston as Loki, Owen Wilson as Mobius, Sophia Di Martino as Sylvie.

---

### 16. Moon Knight
*Series · ~4 hours*

- **What** — Marc Spector discovers he is the avatar of the Egyptian moon god Khonshu, while also living with dissociative identity disorder.
- **Why** — Dark magic and divine politics that sit outside the Avengers framework. References to Latveria are subtle but present. Contextually relevant to Doom's world of mystical forces operating beyond the visible MCU.
- **When** — After Loki, before No Way Home.
- **Who** — Oscar Isaac as Marc Spector/Steven Grant/Moon Knight.

---

### 17. Spider-Man: No Way Home ★
*Film · 2h 28m*

- **What** — Peter asks Doctor Strange to cast a spell that makes the world forget who Spider-Man is. The spell goes wrong and opens the door to other universes.
- **Why** — Reality fractures here for the first time as a direct result of human action — no cosmic entity, just a teenager botching a spell. This is the moment the multiverse shifts from theory to threat.
- **When** — After Moon Knight, before MoM.
- **Who** — Tom Holland as Peter Parker, Benedict Cumberbatch as Strange, three generations of Spider-Man.

---

### 18. Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness ★
*Film · 2h 6m*

- **What** — Strange travels through the multiverse to protect America Chavez, while the Scarlet Witch attempts to destroy every version of herself that stands in her way.
- **Why** — The word Incursion is introduced here in earnest for the first time. An Incursion is what happens when two universes physically collide and one or both are destroyed. This is the core of what Doom threatens to cause or exploit in Doomsday. Without understanding this, you will miss the stakes of the film entirely.
- **When** — Directly after No Way Home.
- **Who** — Benedict Cumberbatch as Strange, Elizabeth Olsen as Wanda, Xochitl Gomez as America Chavez.

---

### 19. Ant-Man and the Wasp: Quantumania
*Film · 2h 5m*

- **What** — Scott Lang and his family are pulled into the Quantum Realm and come face to face with Kang the Conqueror, a time-travelling ruler who wants to control the universe.
- **Why** — The Kang storyline has officially been replaced by Doctor Doom as Doomsday's primary antagonist. Even so, Quantumania provides context for the scale of a multiversal power struggle. The concepts overlap with what Doom is building toward.
- **When** — After MoM.
- **Who** — Paul Rudd as Scott Lang, Jonathan Majors as Kang.

---

### 20. Black Panther: Wakanda Forever ★
*Film · 2h 41m*

- **What** — Wakanda mourns T'Challa while a new threat emerges from the underwater world of Talokan, led by the immortal Namor.
- **Why** — Namor is confirmed for Doomsday. His introduction here — his motivations, his protection of Talokan, his willingness to use violence — is essential. Ironheart also makes her debut. Wakanda's politics and its alliance with Talokan factor into the global balance of power heading into Doomsday.
- **When** — After Quantumania.
- **Who** — Letitia Wright as Shuri, Tenoch Huerta as Namor, Dominique Thorne as Ironheart.

---

### 21. The Marvels
*Film · 1h 45m*

- **What** — Carol Danvers, Kamala Khan and Monica Rambeau are linked through their powers and forced to work together while involuntarily swapping places.
- **Why** — Universes begin leaking here. The post-credits scene introduces a direct X-Men connection that explains Doomsday's broader cast. The lightest film in this block, but the multiverse consequences are real.
- **When** — After Wakanda Forever.
- **Who** — Brie Larson as Carol Danvers, Teyonah Parris as Monica, Iman Vellani as Kamala Khan.

---

### 22. Deadpool & Wolverine ★
*Film · 2h 7m*

- **What** — Wade Wilson is recruited by the TVA and travels through the Void — a graveyard of dead timelines — alongside a variant version of Logan.
- **Why** — The most direct Doomsday preparation in terms of terminology. The concept of Anchor Beings — figures whose existence keeps a timeline alive — is fully worked out here. It also officially connects the Fox universe to the MCU, making the return of the original X-Men cast in Doomsday possible.
- **When** — Closing out Block 3.
- **Who** — Ryan Reynolds as Deadpool, Hugh Jackman as Wolverine.

## Lore & Concepts

A reference guide to the most important elements, objects and mechanisms in the MCU. Everything you need to understand before Doomsday begins.

---

### Vibranium

Vibranium is a rare metal found exclusively in Wakanda, where it arrived via a meteorite millions of years ago. It absorbs and redirects kinetic energy rather than dispersing it — making it virtually indestructible and simultaneously an inexhaustible energy source. Captain America's shield is made of Vibranium. The entire technological infrastructure of Wakanda — buildings, weapons, medical equipment, the Maglev rail system — is built on it. Vibranium is also what makes Wakanda a global superpower: who owns it and who gets access to it determines geopolitical relationships. In Doomsday, Wakanda is for that reason both a strategic target and a potential ally. Namor's Talokan possesses its own variant — an underwater form of the metal that has sustained his people for centuries.

*See: Black Panther, Avengers: Age of Ultron, Black Panther: Wakanda Forever*

---

### Infinity Stones

The six Infinity Stones are the most powerful objects in the universe — each a concentrated manifestation of a primal force that came into being at the birth of the universe. Whoever possesses all six and wears a gauntlet capable of channelling them can reshape reality at will.

The six stones and their domain:

- **Space Stone** (Tesseract) — teleportation and spatial manipulation
- **Mind Stone** (embedded in Vision's forehead) — consciousness and thought control
- **Reality Stone** (Aether) — altering physical reality
- **Power Stone** — devastating energy on a cosmic scale
- **Time Stone** (Eye of Agamotto) — time manipulation and foresight
- **Soul Stone** — control over the living and the dead; requires the sacrifice of a loved one

Thanos uses all six in Infinity War to destroy half of all life. In Endgame they are retrieved through time travel to undo the Snap, after which Tony Stark uses them to destroy Thanos and his army — sacrificing himself in the process. After Endgame, the stones in the current timeline have been destroyed by Thanos. In other timelines and universes, variants still exist.

*See: The Avengers, Avengers: Infinity War, Avengers: Endgame*

---

### Sokovia

Sokovia is a fictional Eastern European country that becomes the epicentre of catastrophe twice due to Avengers involvement. In Age of Ultron, Ultron physically lifts an entire section of the city off the ground — and lets it crash back down like a meteor, killing thousands. The Sokovia Accords are the direct political response: an international treaty requiring superhumans to operate under UN oversight. The treaty splits the Avengers in Civil War — Stark supports it, Rogers refuses. The division that follows directly contributes to the Avengers' failure against Thanos in Infinity War. Sokovia is not a location — it is the fracture.

*See: Avengers: Age of Ultron, Captain America: Civil War*

---

### Wakanda

Wakanda is a nation-state in sub-Saharan Africa that has concealed itself for centuries behind a holographic façade of poverty and insignificance. In reality it is the most technologically advanced civilisation on Earth, powered by Vibranium. Under T'Challa, Wakanda opens its borders to the world for the first time at the end of Black Panther — a geopolitical seismic shift. In Infinity War, Wakanda offers its territory as a battlefield, and loses. In Endgame, Wakanda's army emerges through Doctor Strange's portals. After T'Challa's death, Shuri inherits the technological and political legacy. In Doomsday, Wakanda is one of the few powers on Earth capable of opening its own front — which makes it an inevitable target.

*See: Black Panther, Avengers: Infinity War, Avengers: Endgame, Black Panther: Wakanda Forever*

---

### Quantum Realm

The Quantum Realm is a subatomic dimension accessible via Pym Particles — a technology developed by Hank Pym. Different laws of physics apply inside the Quantum Realm: distance and time are relative, and large amounts of energy can be stored and released in ways that are impossible in the normal world. The Quantum Realm is the key to Endgame's time travel — Scott Lang discovers that time loops can be traversed through it. It is also the dimension where Janet van Dyne is trapped for thirty years, and where Kang the Conqueror has built his empire in Quantumania. Conceptually, the Quantum Realm is the most accessible bridge between the normal world and the broader multiverse structure — which makes it strategically significant for anyone looking to manipulate timelines.

*See: Ant-Man and the Wasp, Avengers: Endgame, Ant-Man and the Wasp: Quantumania*

---

### The TVA — Time Variance Authority

The TVA is a bureaucratic organisation existing outside the normal flow of time that monitors the Sacred Timeline — the one "approved" timeline that must be maintained. Timelines that deviate are flagged as "Nexus Events" and neutralised by TVA agents. Variants — versions of people from other timelines — are arrested, judged and reset. Behind the TVA lies a far darker system: the organisation was set up by He Who Remains, a variant of Kang the Conqueror, to prevent his more dangerous variants from seizing power. When Sylvie kills him at the end of Loki Season 1, the Sacred Timeline shatters into endless branches. Season 2 ends with Loki personally holding the entire timeline structure together — literally the spider at the centre of a web of all possible histories. That position makes him one of the most crucial players in Doomsday.

*See: Loki S1 & S2*

---

### Incursions

An Incursion is what happens when two parallel universes physically collide. This occurs when people travel through the multiverse too frequently or make too many alterations — the boundaries between universes become increasingly porous until they break. During an Incursion, the two Earths of the affected universes crash into each other, resulting in the destruction of one or both universes. The concept is first explained in Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness by the Illuminati of Earth-838. The Scarlet Witch's travels through the multiverse have caused Incursions. In the context of Doomsday, this is the mechanism that Doctor Doom may be exploiting or attempting to control — and it is the reason multiple universes and timelines are simultaneously at stake.

*See: Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness*

---

### Anchor Beings

An Anchor Being is a person whose existence is so fundamentally bound to a specific timeline that the timeline cannot survive without them. If an Anchor Being dies, the timeline begins to disintegrate — fading and eventually disappearing entirely. The concept is introduced in Deadpool & Wolverine in the context of the TVA's management of timelines. Deadpool is the Anchor Being of his own timeline. This has direct implications for Doomsday: if Doctor Doom wants to destroy timelines, he does not need to annihilate an entire universe — he only needs to eliminate the Anchor Being. Conversely, it makes Anchor Beings the most protected and the most endangered individuals in a multiversal war.

*See: Deadpool & Wolverine*

---

### The Multiverse

The multiverse is the totality of all parallel universes, timelines and alternate Earths that exist alongside one another. Every moment a choice plays out differently, a time traveller intervenes or a Nexus Event occurs, a new branch can form. In the early MCU phases the multiverse exists as a concept but remains dormant. The TVA keeps it in check. That changes when Sylvie kills He Who Remains — after which endless timelines emerge. No Way Home, MoM and Deadpool & Wolverine make the multiverse concrete step by step: first as a threat, then as a battlefield, then as a graveyard. In Doomsday, the multiverse is no longer an abstraction — it is the arena.

*See: Loki, Spider-Man: No Way Home, Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness, Deadpool & Wolverine*

### The Mind Stone & Vision

The Mind Stone is one of the six Infinity Stones — the stone governing consciousness and thought control. Hydra uses it to give Wanda and Pietro Maximoff their powers. Stark and Banner use it to create Ultron, and then to bring Vision to life — the Mind Stone literally becomes Vision's core, the source of his consciousness and power. In Infinity War, Thanos tears the Mind Stone from Vision's forehead, causing his death. In WandaVision, Wanda creates a new Vision out of grief — but without the stone. White Vision, created by SWORD, recovers his memories through a logical argument put forward by the "original" Vision, but leaves the scene without emotion or attachment. VisionQuest picks up directly from here: who is Vision without the Mind Stone? Can a consciousness be authentic without its original core?

*See: Avengers: Age of Ultron, Avengers: Infinity War, WandaVision, VisionQuest*

### Doctor Doom — Victor von Doom

Victor von Doom is one of the most complex and dangerous antagonists ever written in Marvel Comics. He is simultaneously a scientific genius, a master of the mystic arts and the absolute ruler of the fictional European kingdom of Latveria. His face is disfigured — by his own account from an explosion, but in reality out of vanity, after he put on a mask too quickly before a wound could heal. The mask is his identity. In the MCU, Doom is played by Robert Downey Jr. — which means he wears the face of Tony Stark. Whether this is a deliberate choice by Doom, a multiversal coincidence or a deeper narrative construction is the central mystery of Doomsday. In the comics, Doom is known for his conviction that he is the only one capable of saving the world — not out of malice, but from a ruthless belief in his own superiority.

*See: The Fantastic Four: First Steps, Avengers: Doomsday*

### The Sokovia Accords & The Political Framework

Following the destruction of Sokovia, the international community forces the Avengers to operate under UN oversight through the Sokovia Accords. Stark supports it — he feels the weight of his responsibility. Rogers refuses — he does not trust a political structure with life and death decisions. This ideological split divides the team in Captain America: Civil War and plays a direct role in why the Avengers are fractured and weakened when Thanos arrives. In Brave New World, Sam Wilson wrestles with a similar tension: what does it mean to be Captain America within a political system that is not always just? The question of whether superhumans should self-regulate or require external oversight runs as a thread through the entire Multiverse Saga era.

*See: Captain America: Civil War, Falcon & The Winter Soldier, Captain America: Brave New World*
